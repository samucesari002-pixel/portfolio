Strategy · Market Analysis · AI Roadmap

CSMT

From industrial network diagnostics to Reliability-as-a-Service — a full strategic analysis of how CSMT can scale its expertise into a recurring revenue model.

Technologies in Action · Cattolica Market Analysis Competitive Positioning AI Roadmap Business Model Design Go-to-Market Strategy
The Problem
Factories are flying blind.

Unplanned industrial downtime costs Fortune 500 companies $1.5 trillion per year. A single production stoppage in automotive can cost up to €50k per minute. Yet most factories have no continuous visibility into the health of the communication networks that keep their machines running.

CSMT — Italy's sole accredited PI Competence & Training Center for PROFIBUS and PROFINET — has the expertise to fix this. The challenge is that their current model is reactive, project-based, and hard to scale.

€50kper minute downtime cost
67%of Italian industrial networks on PROFINET
1,600+clients across 20+ years
The Approach
Eight chapters. One strategic thesis.

Working directly with CSMT as a real client, our team conducted a structured analysis across eight dimensions: the problem space, the company, the market, the technology, an AI integration roadmap, the business model, the go-to-market strategy, and the phased outcome.

We identified five converging market tailwinds — from the $15.2B fieldbus market to IEC62443 cybersecurity standards — all pointing toward an independent protocol specialist like CSMT. The analysis showed that CSMT's PNT Online software is not just a monitoring tool: it is the data pipeline for a predictive intelligence platform.

Competitor analysis against HMS/Procentec, Softing, and Indu-Sol confirmed that CSMT is the only player combining vendor-neutrality, software, consulting, training, and Italian market presence in a single offering.

The AI Roadmap
4 layers. 2 deployable now.

We mapped a four-layer AI integration path on top of CSMT's existing PNT Online data infrastructure — which already collects ~300M data points per plant per year.

L1 — Universal Rules: detect standard network issues. Ready now.

L2 — Anomaly Detection: identify abnormal behaviour patterns. Ready now.

L3 — Predictive Maintenance: anticipate failures before they happen. Near-term.

L4 — Troubleshooting Suggestions: guided AI-driven resolution. Future.

The key insight: AI is the enabler of the business model, not the product itself.

The Business Model
Reliability-as-a-Service. Four tiers.

T1 — Network Health Check: audit, risk assessment, prioritised actions. Entry point, deployable now.

T2 — Continuous Monitoring: PNT Online dashboards, alerts, annual subscription. Core recurring revenue.

T3 — Predictive Reliability: anomaly detection and pattern recognition. Near-term unlock.

T4 — Reliability Partner: SLAs, strategic advisory, uptime insurance. Premium tier.

Priority targets: Automotive, Food & Beverage/Packaging, Machine Builders, Northern Italy SMEs.

The Outcome
Start lean, scale smart.

We recommended a three-phase commercialisation strategy: 0–12 months focused on commercial validation through 5–10 proof-of-concepts and 2–3 integrator partnerships; 12–36 months converting PoCs into T2 subscriptions and expanding the Northern Italy channel; 36+ months scaling predictive models and entering EU markets. The go-to-market entry point is the Health Check — evidence built from the customer's own plant, removing the barrier of proving value before failure happens.

My contribution
My role Competitive analysis (HMS/Procentec, Softing, Indu-Sol) and business model design — mapping how CSMT's current reactive model could transition to a scalable service architecture.

AI Systems · Prompt Engineering

Negocierge

A cross-cultural negotiation AI agent — built from a 129-reference knowledge base through to structured system instructions, and tested live on a real international case.

AI Agent Design System Instructions Knowledge Base Architecture Cross-Cultural Frameworks Prompt Engineering
The Problem
Cultural gaps sink deals that should close.

International negotiations fail not because the deal economics don't work, but because the people across the table are operating from different cultural assumptions — about time, hierarchy, trust, directness, and what "yes" actually means.

Commercial managers rarely have a structured way to prepare for these gaps. The goal was to build an AI agent that could close this gap: giving any manager a concrete, actionable cultural and negotiation brief before and during a cross-cultural deal.

The Approach
Seven layers. One coherent system.

We designed the agent architecture across seven structured components: Role, Context, Knowledge Base Usage rules, Output Criteria, Core Instructions, Output Format specifications, and worked Examples.

The knowledge base was built from 9 sources and 129 individually tracked references, organised into three pillars: negotiation methodology (BATNA, ZOPA, concession sequencing), analytical frameworks (Hofstede, Meyer's Culture Map, Trompenaars), and empirically observed regional patterns for East Asia, Latin America, the Arab world, Sub-Saharan Africa, and Northern Europe.

The Case Study
Project Amazonia-Hydro.

We tested the agent on a live scenario: a €45M joint venture between H2-Lübeck GmbH (a German green-hydrogen engineering firm) and Agropalma S.A. (a Brazilian agricultural enterprise) to build a solar-powered hydrogen facility in Mato Grosso.

The negotiation had two critical friction points: a timeline conflict (the German director perceived the Brazilian counterpart as disorganised; the Brazilian perceived the German as cold and rigid) and a governance clash (70/30 voting split demanded by Germany vs. 50/50 joint board insisted on by Brazil).

The agent produced a cultural profile, stakeholder map, risk analysis, and a step-by-step communication strategy for de-escalation — grounded in Hofstede's power distance and Meyer's Trusting scale.

What I Built
Rigour built into the rules.

The system instructions include hard negative checks — the agent can never produce absolute statements about cultural behaviour, can never generate tactically empty cultural profiles, and must always name at least one cultural risk the user didn't raise themselves.

Output format is fixed: cultural profile → stakeholder map → risks → pressure-testing moves → offer letter. Depth calibrates automatically to the user's declared seniority level: senior managers receive direct recommendations; junior managers receive the reasoning first.

The agent also handles edge cases explicitly: mid-negotiation re-entry, stereotyping pushback, contradictory information, and out-of-scope requests.

9knowledge base sources
129tracked references
7instruction layers
The Outcome
An agent that knows when to push back.

Negocierge is designed to challenge the user's assumptions, not just confirm them. When a user says "Arabs always negotiate like that," the agent qualifies the generalisation before acting on it — and asks what specific behaviour they've actually observed. The output is always tied to a concrete negotiation move, never cultural description for its own sake. The result is a tool that functions as a genuine sparring partner, not a country-fact lookup.

Business Process Redesign

Canton Wonton

A complete 8-step process redesign of a Chinese restaurant — from sub-process mapping and root cause analysis through to a RACI matrix and a 6-week change management plan.

Process Mapping Root Cause Analysis Benchmarking RACI Matrix Change Management KPI Design
The Problem
45 minutes to serve a table. The problem isn't the people.

The Canton Wonton restaurant was experiencing chronic service delays — total cycle time exceeded 45 minutes per table, with the ordering and kitchen coordination phase alone taking 25.7 minutes. Table turnover was constrained, staff were overloaded, and customer satisfaction was declining.

The key insight from our analysis: the problem lies in process design, not in individual performance. Waiters were handling order-taking, delivery, billing, and kitchen coordination simultaneously. The dishwasher was covering drinks, table cleaning, and dishwashing. No digital order system existed. Communication between front-of-house and kitchen was entirely manual.

45+min total cycle time
25.7min ordering phase alone
8redesign steps
The Approach
8 steps. Prepare, focus, design, configure.

Step 1 — Prepare: assembled the redesign team with representatives from each role (manager, maître, cook, waiter, dishwasher).

Step 2 — Focus & Select: identified the 7 key customer satisfaction drivers via QR surveys, paper feedback, online reviews monitoring, and internal observation.

Step 3 — Sub-process Mapping: built a full swim-lane diagram across all five roles, mapping every hand-off from customer entry to exit.

Step 4 — Root Cause Analysis: identified 4 structural causes: unclear task distribution, lack of digital communication, long process chain, and understaffing on peak days (Friday).

Steps 5–6 — Benchmarking & Solutions: benchmarked against Wagamama, McDonald's, a KDS-equipped restaurant, and other Canton Wonton locations. Proposed three solutions: Cloud KDS, Smart Payment via QR, and a digital training programme.

Steps 7–8 — Configure: produced a full RACI matrix and a 6-week change management timeline with key risks per activity.

The Outcome
Structure over blame.

The redesign package included a digitised ordering system eliminating manual kitchen communication, a smart QR payment system reducing the 12-minute payment phase, a layout redesign with dedicated beverage station and pass-through, a RACI reassigning task ownership across all roles, and a phased 6-week implementation plan with milestones from technical setup through KPI analysis. The project demonstrated that operational problems are almost always systemic — and that clarity of roles and communication flows is the first lever to pull.

My role Sub-process mapping — building the swim-lane diagram across all five roles (client, maître, waiter, dishwasher, cook) and mapping every hand-off from entry to exit.

Project Management

The Journey

A complete project plan for the €315k renewal of an assembly workshop in Shanghai — WBS, critical path, S-curve cost model, and a Go/No-Go recommendation delivered within budget and schedule.

WBS · Work Breakdown Structure Critical Path Analysis Cost Modelling · S-Curve Risk Management RACI · Governance Go / No-Go Decision
The Brief
Modernise the Shanghai line. Don't stop production for more than 4 weeks.

Shandong Productions — a Shanghai-based manufacturer — needed to renovate its assembly workshop as part of a "Move Up Our Brand" strategic programme. The mandate: increase capacity by 30%, cut energy consumption by 20%, install a new HVAC climate-control system, and do it in 21 weeks with a maximum budget of €315k (plus €35k management reserve).

The project covered: replacing 5 assembly machines with new-generation units, a turn-key first-of-type HVAC system, roof insulation, 15 high-efficiency windows, and full electrical/fluids/conveyor re-routing.

€315ktotal project budget
21 wkscalendar duration
4,050htotal effort
The Plan
Four periods. One S-curve.

Period 1 — Foundation & Design (W1–W9): initiation, planning, and full engineering design. €48.3k spend, 968h effort. Milestones: plan approved, design complete.

Period 2 — Sourcing & Site Prep (W9–W11): procurement of all machinery, HVAC, and materials; workshop cleared. €150.4k — the procurement spike representing 57% of total budget in 2 weeks. Production stop begins.

Period 3 — Installation (W11–W16): facilities, machinery, conveyors, and control software installed. €79.2k, 1,720h — the most labour-intensive phase. Production stop ends at W14 (4 weeks exactly, within the constraint).

Period 4 — Testing & Handover (W17–W19): commissioning, validation, staff training, and final acceptance. €37k, 756h. Go-live on 18 June.

The S-curve cost model shows the characteristic procurement spike at W10, the CNY plateau, and a smooth final burn. The €35k management reserve was held at project level, released only by the sponsor — protecting against the first-of-type HVAC risk.

Governance
Clear accountability from day one.

The project charter defined four governance roles with explicit RAM accountability: Project Sponsor (budget approval, Go/No-Go authority), Project Manager (plan, schedule, cost, risk), Technical Manager (authority over all engineering work), and Procurement Manager (Yangtze machinery and HVAC vendor contracts).

Each period had a named accountable lead and a milestone-based handoff structure, ensuring no phase could begin without the previous one formally closed.

The Verdict
GO — recommended.

Every success criterion was met by design. Capacity +30% through new-generation machinery. Energy −20% via HVAC, insulation, and windows. Climate at 20°C ±2°C through turn-key HVAC. Cost at exactly €315k — on budget. Duration at 19 working weeks + 2 CNY = 21 calendar weeks — on schedule. Production interruption at 4 weeks — within the constraint.

Our recommendation to the Sponsor: GO. The plan is sound, all targets are satisfied, and the €35k reserve provides adequate protection for the first-of-type HVAC risk.

What This Showed
Planning is a decision-making tool, not paperwork.

The value of this project wasn't the Gantt chart — it was the Go/No-Go decision at the end. By building a rigorous plan with explicit constraints, cost modelling, risk allocation, and governance structure, we gave the Sponsor something actionable: a clear recommendation they could stand behind. That is what good project management produces — not documents, but decisions with confidence behind them.

My contribution
My role Effort matrix — assigning and balancing workload across all project roles and phases, ensuring resource allocation aligned with the 21-week schedule and €315k cost constraint.
